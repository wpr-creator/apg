The Federal Bureaucracy

What it does and why it matters
4 Core Functions
1. Implementation — Carrying out laws Congress passes (EPA enforces clean air laws).

2. Rulemaking — Writing specific regulations that have the force of law. Congress sets broad goals; agencies fill in the details.

3. Adjudication — Agencies resolve disputes about whether rules were violated — like a mini court system.

4. Service delivery — Actually providing government services (Social Security checks, student loans, veterans' benefits).
Who Controls the Bureaucracy?
Congress — creates agencies, funds them, holds oversight hearings.
President — appoints agency heads, issues executive orders, controls budget via OMB.
Courts — can strike down agency actions that violate law or rights.
Interest groups — lobby agencies constantly; key to iron triangles.

Iron Triangles

The most-tested bureaucracy concept
What It Is
An iron triangle is a stable, mutually beneficial three-way relationship between a congressional subcommittee, a federal agency, and an interest group — all in the same policy area. Each helps the others, making them powerful and hard to break up.
How Each Corner Helps the Others
Subcommittee → Agency: Budget funding and favorable legislation
Agency → Subcommittee: Expertise, info, helps members claim credit for policy wins
Interest Group → Subcommittee: Campaign contributions, voter mobilization, expert testimony
Subcommittee → Interest Group: Passes favorable laws and regulatory decisions
Agency → Interest Group: Writes favorable regulations
Interest Group → Agency: Political support and defense from budget cuts
Classic Example + Issue Networks
Defense Iron Triangle: Armed Services Committee + Department of Defense + defense contractors. Each feeds the others.

Issue Networks (modern alternative) — Looser, more fluid webs of actors: academics, think tanks, journalists, advocacy groups, bureaucrats. No permanent deal. More open to change than iron triangles.

Practice Q1 — Identify the Concept

The EPA writes detailed pollution limits after Congress passes the Clean Air Act. Congress only set broad goals — the EPA fills in the specifics with binding rules.
Bureaucratic rulemaking — Congress passes broad laws; agencies write specific binding regulations. This is one of the core functions of bureaucracy: translating legislative intent into enforceable rules. The EPA's pollution limits have the force of law even though Congress never voted on the specific numbers.

Practice Q2 — Iron Triangle

A Senate agriculture subcommittee consistently gives the USDA favorable budget increases. Farm lobbyists donate heavily to those senators and provide expert testimony. The USDA writes regulations benefiting large agricultural corporations.
Iron triangle — Three corners, all helping each other: Senate agriculture subcommittee (budget/legislation) ↔ USDA (favorable regulations) ↔ agricultural interest groups (campaign contributions and expertise). Every corner benefits. This is the textbook example.

Practice Q3 — Congressional Oversight

Congress holds hearings investigating whether the Department of Veterans Affairs mismanaged funds and calls agency officials to testify under oath.
Congressional oversight — Congress has the power to investigate and monitor executive branch agencies through hearings, subpoenas, and the power of the purse. This is a key check Congress holds over the bureaucracy. It's also how iron triangles can be disrupted — public hearings expose the cozy relationships between agencies and interest groups.

Practice Q4 — Iron Triangle vs. Issue Network

A loose coalition of climate scientists, environmental think tanks, journalists, advocacy organizations, and some EPA staffers all influence climate policy. Their relationships shift depending on the issue, and no stable exclusive deal connects them.
Issue network — Unlike iron triangles (stable, exclusive, three-corner), issue networks are fluid and open. They include a wider range of actors with varying influence. No permanent deal — just a complex web of people engaged in the same policy area. More democratic but also less predictable than iron triangles.

Practice Q5 — Apply to FRQ

Essay prompt: "Iron triangles represent a failure of democratic accountability in the American system." Write one sentence connecting iron triangles to this argument.
Iron triangles — the stable, mutually reinforcing relationships between congressional subcommittees, federal agencies, and interest groups — represent a potential democratic accountability failure because policy in those domains is effectively made by three insulated actors rather than the broader public, insulating regulatory decisions from electoral pressure.

Due Process — 5th & 14th Amendments

Federal vs. State — and two different types
The Core Distinction
5th Amendment — "No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process." Limits the federal government.

14th Amendment — "No state shall deprive any person of life, liberty, or property without due process." Limits state governments. Ratified 1868 after the Civil War.
The 5th protects you from Washington. The 14th protects you from your state government. Same words, different targets.
Procedural vs. Substantive
Procedural Due Process — The government must follow fair procedures: notice, a hearing, an impartial decision-maker. It's about HOW the government acts.

Substantive Due Process — Some rights are so fundamental the government cannot take them away AT ALL — even with perfect procedures. It's about WHAT the government can do.

Selective Incorporation

Applying the Bill of Rights to the states — one case at a time
How It Works
The Bill of Rights originally only limited the federal government. The Supreme Court uses the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause to apply specific rights to the states — but only when it decides a right is "fundamental to ordered liberty."

Required cases that ARE incorporation examples:
• Gideon v. Wainwright (1963) — 6th Amendment right to counsel
• Engel v. Vitale (1962) — 1st Amendment Establishment Clause
• McDonald v. Chicago (2010) — 2nd Amendment right to bear arms
• Tinker v. Des Moines (1969) — 1st Amendment free speech in schools

Equal Protection — 14th Amendment

Three levels of scrutiny — know which applies when
The Three Tiers
Strict Scrutiny — Race, national origin, religion, alienage. Compelling interest + narrowly tailored. Government almost always loses. → Brown v. Board, Shaw v. Reno

Intermediate Scrutiny — Gender. Important interest + substantially related means. Government sometimes wins.

Rational Basis — Everything else (age, wealth, disability). Just needs a legitimate purpose. Government almost always wins.
Memory trick: Strict = almost always struck down. Rational basis = almost always upheld. Intermediate = in between.

Practice Q1 — Procedural Due Process

A city fires a public school teacher without giving her advance notice, a chance to respond, or any hearing of any kind.
Procedural due process violation — Before the government takes away a property interest (a job), it must follow fair procedures: notice and opportunity to be heard. Firing without any process violates the 14th Amendment's procedural due process guarantee against state governments.

Practice Q2 — Levels of Scrutiny

A state passes a law restricting voting rights for people who have ever been convicted of a misdemeanor. Affected citizens challenge it under the Equal Protection Clause.
Rational basis — The three suspect classifications for strict scrutiny are race, national origin, and religion (plus alienage). Criminal history is not a suspect classification. Laws affecting non-suspect groups get rational basis review — the government just needs a legitimate purpose, like protecting election integrity. Government almost always wins under this standard.

Practice Q3 — Selective Incorporation

Florida charges a man with felony theft. He cannot afford a lawyer. Under Gideon v. Wainwright, the state must provide one because:
B is correct — and this is the key. The 6th Amendment alone didn't originally apply to states. Gideon v. Wainwright is an incorporation case — the Court used the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause to incorporate the 6th Amendment right to counsel against the states. Always connect Gideon to both the 6th AND 14th Amendments.

Practice Q4 — Equal Protection Classic

A state law segregates public school students by race, providing separate but ostensibly equal facilities. Under Brown v. Board of Education:
Brown v. Board held that separate is inherently unequal — even with equal physical facilities, the very act of separation sends a message of inferiority that violates equal protection. This overturned Plessy v. Ferguson (1896). It applies strict scrutiny to racial classifications, which the state almost never survives.

Practice Q5 — 5th vs. 14th Amendment

A federal agency seizes a person's property without any notice or hearing. vs. A state court convicts a person without providing a lawyer. Which amendment is the primary basis for each challenge?
B is the key distinction. The 5th Amendment limits the FEDERAL government — the federal agency seizure is a 5th Amendment issue. The 14th Amendment limits STATE governments — the state court case is a 14th Amendment issue (due process incorporating the 6th Amendment right to counsel). This distinction is exactly what the exam tests.

Linkage Institutions — The Four Bridges

What connects citizens to government
The Four Types
1. Political Parties — Recruit candidates, mobilize voters, organize government. Connect citizens to elections through clear choices.

2. Interest Groups — Organize citizens with shared goals and advocate for them. Lobby Congress, mobilize members, litigate, contribute via PACs.

3. Elections — Primary mechanism for citizen accountability over government.

4. Media — Sets the agenda, acts as watchdog, connects government and citizens through information.

Interest Groups — The Most Tested

What they do, how they do it, and the free-rider problem
What Interest Groups Actually Do
Lobbying — Directly contacting members of Congress or agency officials to advocate for positions.

Electioneering — Contributing to candidates via PACs or Super PACs (after Citizens United), endorsing candidates, mobilizing members to vote.

Litigation — Filing lawsuits or amicus curiae (friend of the court) briefs to influence courts. ACLU, NAACP LDF are examples.

Grassroots mobilization — Organizing members to contact officials, attend town halls, and vote.
The Free-Rider Problem
People benefit from interest group wins without joining. If the Sierra Club wins clean air regulations, everyone benefits — not just members. This creates a rational incentive to let others do the work. Groups overcome it with selective benefits — perks only members get.
Pluralism vs. Elite Theory
Pluralism — Policy is made through competition among interest groups; none dominates. This is Federalist No. 10's vision applied to modern politics.

Elite theory — A small, wealthy elite uses interest groups to dominate policy outcomes — real pluralism doesn't exist.

Practice Q1 — Type of Interest Group Activity

The NAACP files an amicus curiae brief in a Supreme Court case arguing that a state's redistricting plan discriminates against Black voters.
Litigation — Interest groups frequently use courts as a political arena. An amicus curiae brief is a formal legal filing where a non-party organization shares its perspective with the court. The NAACP LDF has used litigation strategically for decades — including in Brown v. Board of Education.

Practice Q2 — Linkage Institutions and Turnout

18–29 year old voters turn out at 27%; voters 50–64 turn out at 63%. Interest groups targeting older voters spend three times more on mobilization than those targeting younger voters.
Linkage institutions and selective mobilization — Interest groups connect citizens to government, but they don't do it equally. By concentrating resources on high-turnout groups, they reinforce the participation gap. Who gets linked to government depends on who gets organized — and organizations follow resources, not civic need.

Practice Q3 — Citizens United + Linkage

After Citizens United v. FEC (2010), a Super PAC spends $50 million on ads supporting a Senate candidate without coordinating with the campaign. This demonstrates interest groups acting as linkage institutions through:
Independent expenditures after Citizens United — The key is 'independent' — Super PACs cannot coordinate with the campaign. Citizens United held that this spending is protected 1st Amendment speech. This is electioneering — interest groups influencing elections — which is one of the core ways they function as linkage institutions connecting concentrated wealth to political outcomes.

Practice Q4 — Pluralism vs. Elite Theory

A political scientist argues: "Defense contractors, pharmaceutical companies, and financial institutions consistently get favorable policies because they can afford expensive lobbying and campaign contributions that ordinary citizens cannot match."
Elite theory — The scenario describes systematic dominance by wealthy, well-resourced groups rather than equal competition. Pluralism would say all groups compete and balance each other out (Madison's Federalist 10 vision). Elite theory says the playing field isn't level and a small group consistently wins. Knowing this distinction lets you argue either side of a FRQ prompt about interest group influence.

Practice Q5 — FRQ Application

Essay prompt: "Interest groups serve as effective linkage institutions in American democracy." Write one sentence using evidence to support OR refute this claim.
Support version: Interest groups serve as effective linkage institutions by translating the shared concerns of citizens — from environmental protection to gun rights to labor conditions — into organized political pressure through lobbying, litigation, and voter mobilization, connecting policy preferences that might otherwise go unrepresented to the formal institutions of government.

Refute version: While interest groups function as linkage institutions in theory, their effectiveness is distributed unequally — well-funded groups representing corporations and wealthy constituencies consistently outspend and outlobby groups representing ordinary citizens, making the linkage they provide more reflective of concentrated economic power than broad democratic preferences.
