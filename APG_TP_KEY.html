FRQ 1

Concept Application

Model structure: describe the concept, connect it to the scenario, then extend the answer to a related institution or broader effect.

Question 1 · Federalism

Federal-State Conflict

FEDERALISM is the division of power between national and state governments. State marijuana legalization while federal law still bans it shows federalism in action. The SUPREMACY CLAUSE could allow federal law to override state law, and federal courts could resolve the conflict.

Question 2 · Executive Power

Executive Order

An EXECUTIVE ORDER is a presidential directive that carries the force of law inside the executive branch. The president acts, Congress responds, and courts review the order, showing checks and balances. JUDICIAL REVIEW could stop the order if unconstitutional.

Question 3 · Socialization

Belief Formation

POLITICAL SOCIALIZATION is how people develop political beliefs. Religious attendance and politically active households show religion and family shaping views. The MEDIA can also influence attitudes by shaping how people understand issues.

3 / 3
Question 4 · Turnout

Rational Abstention

RATIONAL ABSTENTION happens when people choose not to vote because they believe their vote will not matter. Younger voters saying their vote “doesn’t matter” explains lower turnout. Interest groups act as LINKAGE INSTITUTIONS by mobilizing voters.

FRQ 2

Quantitative Analysis

Model structure: describe data, explain the pattern using an AP concept, then connect the pattern to political behavior or strategy.

Question 1 · Voter Turnout

Age & Participation

The 60–74 group has the highest turnout at 76%. Turnout generally rises with age, from 50% among 18–29 year-olds to higher levels in older groups. POLITICAL SOCIALIZATION helps explain stronger civic habits among older voters. Parties and interest groups would focus outreach on reliable older voters.

4 / 4
Question 2 · Party ID

Generational Divide

Younger generations lean more Democratic, while older generations lean more Republican. POLITICAL SOCIALIZATION explains differences because beliefs are shaped by formative experiences, family, media, and major events. Parties adjust campaign strategy by targeting different age coalitions.

FRQ 3

SCOTUS Comparison

Model structure: state the required case holding, compare the constitutional issue, apply the rule, and argue the likely outcome.

Question 1 · Tinker

Student Speech

Tinker v. Des Moines held that students have First Amendment rights in school unless speech causes substantial disruption. A post targeting a teacher and encouraging students to ignore work could meet the disruption standard. The Court would likely rule for the school.

4 / 4
Question 2 · McCulloch

Federal Supremacy

McCulloch v. Maryland held that Congress has implied powers under the Necessary and Proper Clause and that states cannot interfere with federal institutions. A national gun database could be valid if necessary to enforce federal law, and state resistance could be overridden by federal supremacy.

FRQ 4

Argumentative Essay Models

Three acceptable positions: support, modify, or refute the claim. Each should include a thesis, evidence, reasoning, and counterargument response.

Support

Checks Work

Separation of powers with checks and balances is effective because each branch has formal powers to limit the others. Federalist No. 51 explains ambition counteracting ambition, and Marbury v. Madison gives courts judicial review.

Modify

Mostly Effective

Checks and balances are generally effective, but they can weaken when one branch becomes too dominant or political loyalty limits accountability. Federalist No. 51 assumes active checking, while United States v. Lopez shows courts can still limit Congress.

Refute

Power Can Expand

Separation of powers is not always effective because political realities can allow one branch or the national government to dominate. Brutus No. 1 warns about centralized power, and McCulloch v. Maryland expanded federal authority.

Civil Rights & Liberties

Due Process, Incorporation & Equal Protection

Core concepts for how the Fourteenth Amendment changed federal-state power and rights protections.

Fifth Amendment

Federal Due Process

Procedural due process requires fair procedures before government takes life, liberty, or property. Substantive due process protects fundamental rights from government interference, even when procedures are followed.

Fourteenth Amendment

State Due Process

The Fourteenth Amendment allows the Supreme Court to apply parts of the Bill of Rights to the states, increasing federal power by letting national courts limit state actions that violate rights.

Selective Incorporation

Rights Applied to States

Selective incorporation is the process by which the Supreme Court applies specific Bill of Rights protections to the states. Examples include Gideon, Engel, Tinker, and McDonald.

Equal Protection

Fair Treatment

The Equal Protection Clause says states cannot deny any person equal protection of the laws. Strict scrutiny applies to race, national origin, and religion; intermediate scrutiny often applies to gender; rational basis applies to most other categories.
