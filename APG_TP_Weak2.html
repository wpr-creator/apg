Federalist No. 78

Alexander Hamilton, 1788
🔴 53% flagged — your weakest document
Central Argument
Hamilton calls the judiciary the "least dangerous branch" — it has neither force (military) nor will (budget). It has only judgment. Because judges have no political power to abuse, they need lifetime tenure to rule based on law rather than political pressure. Crucially, Hamilton argues courts must be able to void laws that contradict the Constitution — the theoretical foundation for judicial review before Marbury v. Madison made it law.
"The judiciary... has no influence over either the sword or the purse... It may truly be said to have neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment."
How to USE It in an FRQ
✅ Argue FOR judicial independence — lifetime tenure protects courts from political pressure
✅ Support judicial review — pair with Marbury v. Madison, which put Hamilton's theory into practice
✅ Argue checks and balances work — courts checking Congress is part of the Federalist 51 framework
❌ Don't use to argue courts have too much power — that's Brutus No. 1's argument

① Argument Challenge

Pick the best way to use Federalist 78 in an FRQ

"The Supreme Court's power of judicial review threatens democratic self-governance because unelected judges can override elected majorities." — Refute using Federalist No. 78.
The key move: use Hamilton's own reasoning — courts have no force or will, only judgment — to argue that judicial review isn't a power grab, it's a structural check with no enforcement muscle behind it. This is what earns the reasoning point: connecting the document's argument to the thesis.

② Which Document Fits?

Match the argument to the right foundational document

Essay prompt: "Congress passes a law expanding presidential war powers. A senator argues the courts should strike it down." Which foundational document best supports the senator's argument?
Federalist 78 is correct — it directly addresses the judiciary's role in voiding unconstitutional laws. The senator is making a judicial review argument, which is Hamilton's core argument in Federalist 78. Pair it with Marbury v. Madison for maximum FRQ impact.

③ Write It

One sentence using Federalist 78 in an FRQ argument

Prompt: "Judicial independence is essential to maintaining the constitutional system of checks and balances."
In Federalist No. 78, Hamilton argued that the judiciary is the "least dangerous branch" precisely because it possesses only judgment — not force or will — making lifetime tenure essential to insulate judicial decisions from political pressure and preserve the Court's ability to check the other branches through judicial review.

Letter from Birmingham Jail

Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., 1963
🔴 51% flagged — second weakest
Central Argument
King argues there are just and unjust laws. An unjust law is one that (1) degrades human personality or (2) is imposed on a minority by a majority that does not bind itself to the same rule. When a law is unjust, it is not just permissible to disobey — it is a moral obligation. But civil disobedience must be nonviolent, open, and willing to accept legal consequences.
"One has not only a legal but a moral responsibility to obey just laws. Conversely, one has a moral responsibility to disobey unjust laws."
How to USE It in an FRQ
✅ Civil rights arguments — legal rights alone are insufficient without moral pressure
✅ Equal protection — laws that apply only to minorities violate King's definition of justice AND the 14th Amendment
✅ Connect to Brown v. Board — King's unjust law = degrades human personality; Brown = segregation is inherently unequal
❌ Don't just say "King believed in civil rights" — name his specific just/unjust distinction and its TWO definitions

① Argument Challenge

"Political participation through established legal channels is sufficient for achieving justice." — Refute using the Letter from Birmingham Jail.
C uses King's specific definition of an unjust law and connects it directly to the prompt. The power move: when the legal channel IS the injustice, you can't use it to fix itself. This directly refutes "legal channels are sufficient."

② Which Document Fits?

Essay prompt argues: "Civil rights progress in America has required citizens to act outside the normal political process." Which foundational document best supports this claim?
Letter from Birmingham Jail — King's entire argument is that when legal channels produce unjust laws, citizens have a moral obligation to disobey through nonviolent direct action. This is the document that directly addresses acting outside the normal political process as a legitimate and necessary form of participation.

③ Write It

Prompt: "The 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause alone was insufficient to produce racial equality in America."
In the Letter from Birmingham Jail (1963), King argued that an unjust law — one imposed on a minority by a majority that does not bind itself to the same rule — carries no moral authority and demands active disobedience, demonstrating that formal legal equality under the 14th Amendment was insufficient to dismantle systems of racial injustice without sustained nonviolent direct action.

Federalist No. 10

James Madison, 1787
🔴 46% flagged
Central Argument
Madison defines a faction as any group driven by self-interest against the rights of others or the common good. Factions can't be eliminated without destroying liberty — so you control their effects. The solution: a large republic. In a large, diverse republic, so many competing factions exist that no single one can dominate. They cancel each other out. Representative government also refines public opinion through elected representatives.
"The latent causes of faction are sown in the nature of man... relief is only to be sought in the means of controlling its effects."
How to USE It in an FRQ
✅ Interest group arguments — interest groups are Madison's factions; the system lets them compete without any one dominating
✅ Defend pluralism — competing interests balancing each other is a feature, not a bug
✅ Argue FOR representative over direct democracy
❌ Don't confuse with Federalist 51 — No. 10 = factions and the extended republic; No. 51 = institutional checks between branches

① Argument Challenge

The NRA, NAACP, Sierra Club, and Chamber of Commerce all lobby Congress and regularly counteract each other's influence. Does this support or undermine Federalist No. 10?
A is correct. Madison didn't think factions were good — he thought they were inevitable and dangerous. His solution was structural: make the republic large enough that no faction can dominate. When interest groups counteract each other, that's exactly the outcome the extended republic was designed to produce. Madison would not have been surprised by modern lobbying — he anticipated it.

② Which Document Fits?

Essay prompt: "Political parties and interest groups threaten to undermine democratic governance by prioritizing narrow interests over the public good." Which foundational document most directly addresses this concern — and from which angle?
Federalist No. 10 — Madison directly addresses the danger of factions (narrow interest groups) and offers a structural solution. You could use it to support the prompt (factions are dangerous, Madison acknowledged it) OR to refute it (the large republic controls their effects). It's the most flexible document for interest group and party arguments.

③ Write It

Prompt: "The American political system has effectively managed competing interests without allowing any single faction to dominate."
In Federalist No. 10, Madison argued that a large, diverse republic controls the dangerous effects of factions by ensuring no single interest group can achieve majority dominance — a structural logic that supports the claim that the American political system, with its many competing interests, tends toward balance rather than factional tyranny.

Brutus No. 1

Anti-Federalist (Robert Yates?), 1787
38% flagged
Central Argument
Brutus argues that a large republic cannot protect liberty — only small communities where citizens know their representatives can do that. The proposed Constitution is dangerous because the Necessary and Proper Clause gives Congress unlimited implied power and the Supremacy Clause will eventually override all state laws. The national government will inevitably expand until it absorbs state authority entirely. This is the direct counter to Federalist No. 10.
"The general government... comprehends every object of human concern... and is capable of being extended to embrace every case that can possibly occur."
How to USE It in an FRQ
✅ States' rights / federalism arguments — when arguing federal power has grown too large
✅ Pair with US v. Lopez — when the Court limited Commerce Clause power, Brutus's warning was finally heeded
✅ Use as a counterargument — if supporting federal power, Brutus is the opposing view to address
❌ Don't confuse with Federalist 51 — Brutus FEARS concentrated power; Fed 51 says the system PREVENTS it

① Argument Challenge

"Federal power expansion since the Founding has come at the cost of the constitutional balance the Framers intended." — Support using Brutus No. 1.
The power move with Brutus: show that his prediction came true. This turns a founding-era document into evidence about a modern pattern. You're not just summarizing Brutus — you're using his argument as a lens on current reality. This earns both the document point AND the reasoning point.

② Federalist 10 vs. Brutus No. 1

These two documents argue opposite sides of the same question

Both Federalist No. 10 and Brutus No. 1 address the question of whether a large republic can protect liberty. Which statement correctly distinguishes their positions?
This contrast is exam gold. Madison (Fed 10): large republic = safety because factions cancel each other. Brutus: large republic = danger because government becomes too distant and powerful. Same question, opposite conclusions. Knowing both sides lets you support OR modify any federalism prompt.

③ Write It

Prompt: "The Anti-Federalists' concerns about concentrated national power have proven more accurate than the Federalists' optimism."
In Brutus No. 1, the Anti-Federalist author warned that the Necessary and Proper and Supremacy Clauses would give the national government virtually unlimited authority to expand its power at the expense of the states — a prediction that the growth of federal programs, Commerce Clause legislation, and implied powers doctrine has largely confirmed over time.

Federalist No. 70

Alexander Hamilton, 1788
36% flagged
Central Argument
Hamilton argues for "energy in the executive" — a strong, single president is essential for decision, activity, secrecy, and dispatch (speed). A single president can act quickly in emergencies, be held accountable by the public, and protect the nation. A plural or weak executive would be too slow and diffuse accountability so no one can be blamed.
"Energy in the executive is a leading character in the definition of good government... a feeble executive implies a feeble execution of the government."
How to USE It in an FRQ
✅ Defend executive orders or unilateral presidential action — Hamilton's energy argument supports acting without waiting for Congress
✅ National security and foreign policy — Hamilton specifically mentions secrecy and speed as executive virtues
❌ Don't confuse with Federalist 51 — No. 70 is specifically about why the EXECUTIVE should be strong; No. 51 is about checks on ALL branches

① Argument Challenge

The president issues an executive order deploying troops without a congressional declaration of war. Critics argue this violates separation of powers. Which argument best uses Federalist No. 70?
Hamilton's argument is fundamentally about why a strong, single executive is valuable — decisiveness and speed. Military deployments require exactly this. Hamilton also emphasized accountability: one president means one person responsible. This directly supports unilateral executive action in time-sensitive situations.

② Federalist 70 vs. Federalist 51 — Know the Difference

An essay prompt argues: "The Founders designed the executive branch to be strong enough to act decisively." Which document best supports this specific claim?
Federalist 70 is the right choice — Hamilton specifically argued that the executive MUST be strong and energetic. Federalist 51 is about checks on all branches, which actually argues the opposite: no branch should become too powerful. When the prompt is specifically about executive strength, reach for Federalist 70.

③ Write It

Prompt: "The unitary executive — a single, powerful president — is essential to effective American governance."
In Federalist No. 70, Hamilton argued that "energy in the executive" — the capacity for decisiveness, secrecy, and speed in a single accountable leader — is essential to effective government, directly supporting the claim that a unitary, powerful executive is a constitutional feature rather than a flaw.

Federalist No. 51

James Madison, 1788
35% flagged
Central Argument
Madison's answer to tyranny is institutional design. "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition." Each branch must have the constitutional tools and personal motivation to resist the others. If men were angels, no government would be necessary — but since they aren't, the structure must do the work. The same logic applies to federalism: national and state governments check each other.
"If men were angels, no government would be necessary... In framing a government which is to be administered by men over men, the great difficulty lies in this: you must first enable the government to control the governed; and in the next place oblige it to control itself."
How to USE It in an FRQ
✅ Any separation of powers argument — this is the foundational defense of checks and balances
✅ Pair with Marbury v. Madison — Fed 51 theorizes judicial independence; Marbury put it into practice
❌ Don't confuse with Federalist 10 — No. 51 = institutional checks between branches; No. 10 = faction control through a large republic

① Argument Challenge

The president vetoes legislation passed by Congress. Congress fails to override the veto. Does this support or undermine Federalist No. 51?
Madison explicitly designed the system so each branch could resist the others. The veto is the executive's check on the legislature. The fact that Congress couldn't override shows the check functioned. This is the system working — ambition (presidential) counteracting ambition (congressional). Exactly what Federalist 51 describes.

② The Big Four — Match the Document

Four Federalist Papers, four different arguments

Match: "The structural design of government — separated powers, checks and balances, and dual sovereignty — prevents any one group from seizing control."
Federalist 51 — the argument is specifically about structural institutional design preventing tyranny. No. 10 also touches this but focuses on factions, not institutional structure. The phrase "ambition must counteract ambition" is the Federalist 51 signature — use it as a signal to grab that document.

③ Write It

Prompt: "The constitutional design of checks and balances effectively prevents any one branch from becoming too powerful."
In Federalist No. 51, Madison argued that "ambition must be made to counteract ambition" — each branch must hold the constitutional means and institutional motivation to resist encroachment by the others — creating a structural framework in which the separated powers system actively prevents single-branch dominance rather than merely hoping for virtue from those in office.
